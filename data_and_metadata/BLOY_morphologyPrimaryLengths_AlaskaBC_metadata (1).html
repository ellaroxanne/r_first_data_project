Description:
Abstract:
These data provide morphological measurements and primary feather length measurements of Black Oystercatcher (Haematopus bachmani) captured at several sites across Alaska and British Columbia between 2019 and 2022. The first table contains mass and morphological measurements of body and bill size. The second table provides lengths of individual primary feathers measured from folded wing photographs using a computer software. These feather lengths are to be used in a size-constrained components analysis to calculate wing shape such as pointedness and convexity.
Purpose:
These data were collected as part of a broader project studying Black Oystercatcher morphology, movement, and diet across Alaska, USA and British Columbia, Canada. Photographs of wings were taken in the field of folded wings on a board with a ruler for scale. The primary length measurements were collected using computer software analysis from wing photos.
Supplemental_Information:
This data release provides only measurement data of adult Black Oystercatchers. Users should be aware that other data or samples from some of the individual birds documented herein may exist. Other data may be maintained by the data collectors, published in reports and scientific articles, or available in other publicly accessible data products, such as U.S. Geological Survey data releases: Black Oystercatcher nest and diet data: https://doi.org/10.5066/F7WH2N5Q and juvenile shorebird morphological data: https://doi.org/10.5066/P9ZH3JNQ.

Process_Step:
Process_Description:
FIELD:
Captures of Black Oystercatchers were conducted in spring and summer in British Columbia (March for spring, May-July for Summer) and summer in Alaska (May-July). Both active and passive capture techniques were employed using standard noose mat and noose line capture methods along with playback and decoys. Upon capture, individuals were aged by plumage and bill color and sexed by the extent of a black fleck in their iris (Guzzetti et al. 2008). Morphological measurements were taken including the length of the unflattened wing chord, tail, middle toe, tarsus, and five bill measurements (exposed culmen length, length of head and bill, bill depth and width at the end of the nares, and depth of the bill tip). Two tarsus measurements were taken for comparison, diagonal tarsus from the interstitial joint to the last leg scale before the toes and maximum tarsus length from the lower hind edge of the tibia to the heel of the foot. Photos were taken of each wing in the folded position, flattened on a board and with a ruler included for scale.
Process_Date: Unknown
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
LAB:
Wing photos were analyzed in the lab to collect lengths of each primary feather using ImageJ software (Schindelin et al. 2012). The primary feathers were measured from the folded wing photos by measuring the difference between the tips of each successive feather. Due to the method of measurement, these estimated measurements include the length of the area between where the feather inserts and the top of the wing. Therefore, these measurements may be longer than others using a different technique and represent a relative primary length rather than a true length of the feather. For convenience, we refer to and label these measurements as primary lengths. Because the 9th primary is the longest, the 10th was never visible in the folded wing photos. As such, we determined the length of the 10th by subtracting the difference between primary 9 and 10 (measured in the field) from the wing length. The length of the wing as measured in the field was used as the 9th primary length. The length of primary 8 was measured by subtracting the difference between feathers 9 and 8 (measured using image) from the length of feather 9, and this process was repeated through the last primary. Due to molt, individuals were often missing several innermost primaries, and primary one was often covered by the secondary feathers. Therefore, only visible primaries and those that appeared to be full length were included in measurements. Each individual’s wing was measured twice and the average between the two estimates was included in this final dataset.
